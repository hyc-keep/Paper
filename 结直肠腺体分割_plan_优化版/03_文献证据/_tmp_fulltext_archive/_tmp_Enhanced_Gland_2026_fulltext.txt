Ponnarasee B. K.1, Lalithamani N.1 & Adeyemi Abel Ajibesin2
Histological image segmentation of colorectal cancer plays a crucial role in reliable cancer grading, 
prognosis evaluation, and treatment planning. Nevertheless, existing automated approaches often 
struggle due to fine-grained glandular structures, staining variations, acquisition differences across 
scanners and highly heterogeneous tissue morphology. To address these challenges, we propose MAC-
Net, an enhanced deep learning model that integrates multi-scale feature fusion with attention-guided 
contextual decoding to achieve precise gland segmentation across diverse tumor differentiation 
stages. MAC-Net retains fine structural information by channel-wise attention, enhances learning of 
discriminative features by the increased number of enriched encoder-decoder lateral connections, 
and the global contextual information, by means of multi-scale spatial pooling at the bottleneck. To 
determine the generalizability of the model, we trained the model on the EBHI-Seg data (2228 images) 
and cross-validated it on the GIaS data (165 images). MAC-Net achieved 95.08% Dice, 90.92% IoU, 
95.83% Precision, and 95.65% Recall, outperforming existing architectures. Furthermore, Gradient-
weighted Class Activation Mapping visualization and uncertainty analysis enhance interpretability, 
provides deeper insight on model’s transparent behavior and supports reliable clinical decision-making 
in the field of digital pathology.

Hybrid loss
Colorectal cancer (CRC) is a fast-growing cancerous disease globally, as it takes the second position in the global 
mortality rate. CRC in India has been posting a gradual, steady rise of between 2%-3% in incidence over the past 
two decades, with a survival rate markedly lower than that in developed countries 1,2. Traditionally, healthcare 
professionals rely on labor-intensive manual histopathological analysis for detection and classification, which 
informs treatment strategies. Nevertheless, manual analysis is prone to misdiagnosis due to subjectivity, inter-
observer variability, and the complexity of human tissue structures 3.The use of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has 
changed the paradigm of pathology by enabling precise diagnoses, simplifying clinical work, and customizing 
treatment. The emergence of Machine Learning (ML) and Deep Learning (DL) has given greater attention to 
digital pathology. The systematic pathological examination of tissue samples is still the gold standard of CRC 
diagnosis4, but AI may be used to aid pathologists in their work, such as tissue segmentation, subtyping of 
cancer, prediction of cancer margins, and survival analysis 5,6. ML/DL-based approaches can be more effective 
and reliable than the manual ones as they enable faster reporting and enhance objectivity in measurement7,8.
Manual gland segmentation is a highly time-consuming and subjective process that requires the expertise 
of well-trained pathologists 3. This explains why automated approaches are necessary to aid cancer diagnosis. 
However, it is still difficult to develop segmentation models that are robust across all the grades of CRC 9. The 
ML approaches are useful in preliminary segmentation and classification, but in cases of severe tissue variation 
and textual inequality, they fail. These complexities, however, can be very well expressed in the DL models 10,11.
Among these, U-Net has been one of the well-known biomedical segmentation models. Its encoder-decoder 
structure with skip connections maintains both global context and fine-scale spatial data that is important in the 
segmentation of complex structures such as glands and tumorous areas12.

The challenges associated with histopathological images of CRC include intricate tissue structure, 
morphological deviations, and slight disparities among tissue classes. The conventional U-Net finds difficulty 
in reflecting these complex patterns because its skip connections are limited in combining low-level spatial and 
high-level semantic features. Therefore, the U-Net results in poor performance on class imbalance segmentation. 
Later studies added UNet++13 and VNet14, which use nested and dense skip connections to enhance localization, 
yet they are still weak at global context. Attention UNet 15 increases the saliency of features via attention gates 
and has difficulty with variability in multi-resolution due to variations in lesion size and shape. In a bid to 
address these shortcomings, hybrid Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) models have been employed, and 
these have resulted in good segmentation in more complex tissue environments. Nevertheless, they have high 
computational overhead due to their greater complexity in architecture, which impedes practical deployment16,17. 
Several challenges to effective segmentation of colorectal histopathology images have thus been identified as the 
need to tackle the existence of varying gland sizes, intra-class variation, delicate inter-tissue variations, and 
staining artifacts.
Our proposed MAC-Net is frameworked with a system of information fusion among various spatial resolutions 
to delineate the boundaries of glands and tumors in the most accurate way 18. It improves representation of 
features by dynamically exploiting informative areas and dropping irrelevant or noisy features, and is resistant 
to background clutter and staining aliasing 19. MAC-Net processes multiple scales of semantic cues to provide 
the larger context, which allows the distinction between precancerous and malignant tissues 20. Transformer-
augmented and prior attention-based models emphasize the importance of enriched context21, although most of 
them focus on one or two of them independently. MAC-Net integrates the spatial, channel-level, and contextual 
features in a single encoder-decoder (U-Net) system as its backbone and optimizes hierarchy to offer robust 
features in segmentation among different stages of CRC. Figure 1 gives a comprehensive overview of the entire 
framework.

The major contributions of this work are summarized as follows, 
 1. Our proposed framework, MAC-Net, a gland segmentation system for colorectal cancer histopathology im-

 2. A context-capturing module is introduced at the core of the network to consolidate the information across 
multiple scales, while on the other hand, the attention mechanisms fine-tune features before fusion in the 
reconstruction path.
 3. We introduce a tailored loss function - an integration of Dice, Focal, and Edge losses to enhance the accuracy

 6. The generalizability of MAC-Net is validated through cross-dataset testing on the GIaS dataset, demonstrat-
ing robustness to unseen histopathological data with varying characteristics.
Related works
This section reviews existing approaches in CRC histopathological segmentation, including CNN-based U-Net 
models, their variants, attention-enhanced models, and hybrid frameworks, with additional focus on emerging 
transformer-based techniques. We also highlight contributions utilizing self-supervised and multi-task learning 
strategies.
CNN and U-Net variants
Sengupta et al.22 conducted a comparative study to evaluate the performance of six CNN-based segmentation 
models - namely, U-Net, Attention U-Net, and U-Net with backbone networks including ResNet50, MobileNetV2, 
EfficientNetB0, and DenseNet121 for gland segmentation in histopathological images. Their finding indicated 
that the U-Net with DenseNet121 and ResNet50 backbones achieved superior segmentation accuracy compared 
to the other variants. Liyu et al.23 introduced the EBHI-Seg dataset for analysis of CRC at various stages - early to 
advanced. They evaluated a host of ML and DL models on the dataset. The precision of the ML methods reached 
up to 70%, whereas the DL methods reached a much higher level (90%).
Attention enhanced and hybrid models
Although the U-Net is a very successful model in extracting local features, it does not always provide the larger 
global context. To overcome this shortcoming, the U-Net is coupled with an Efficient Multi-scale Attention 
(EMA) module 24. This EMA-Swin U-Net model allows the presence of robust feature representation across 
tissue scales. Similarly, the Dynamic Mixture of Convolutions U-Net (DMoC-UNet) 25, uses adaptive expert 
pathways that dynamically adapt to structural variations, improving segmentation of heterogeneous lesion areas.
Transformer based models
Although the number of reported applications is low, recent research has proven the increasing interest in 
Transformer-based models, supported by their ability to process spatially heterogeneous histopathological 
images.26,27. CervixFormer is a Swin Transformer-based classification framework showing excellent performance 
in classifying malignant cells, with Grad-CAM visualizations that were used to interpret the classified images28. 
The models leverage a self-attention mechanism to obtain long-range dependencies in images, which is beneficial 
to task segmenting large and spatially intricate structures in Whole Slide Images (WSI). Minimal Information 
Loss Dilated Network (MILD-Net) is a fully convolutional network that preserves significant information by 
reintroducing the original image and atrous spatial pyramid pooling, thereby discretionary the extraction of 
features on multi-scale level. MILD-Net uses random transformations at test time in order to deal with prediction 
uncertainty, and this improves segmentation and highlights ambiguous regions29.
Multi-task and self-supervised learning
In addition to the supervised methods, self-supervised learning (SSL) has been considered to utilise unlabeled 
CRC data. The multi-resolution SSL framework involves context-target fusion and masked jigsaw tasks modules, 
to promote multi-resolution feature learning and hence leading to the improvement of segmentation of cancer 
datasets over current approaches in WSI 30. Graham et al applied a multi-task learning (MTL) framework for 
the simultaneous segmentation and classification of nuclei, glands, lumina, and various tissue regions in in 
diagnosing CRC. A tailored sampling technique and shared features of the framework enabled them to learn 
from multiple datasets31.
Methodology
This section presents the methodology adopted for developing the MAC-Net framework for gland segmentation 
in CRC histopathological images. MAC-Net uses channel-wise attention, multi-scale feature fusion, and 
contextual information aggregation implied with an encoder-decoder architecture in order to increase the 
precision of segmentation among all stages of cancer and also glandular structures. Each module is discussed 
in detail in the upcoming subsections. The systematic architecture of the proposed model is illustrated in Fig. 2.

MAC-Net architecture
The input of the model is a normalized histopathological image, which is 256 ×256 pixels (px) with 3 color 
channels. T o extract semantic and spatial features, the encoder path has four convolutional blocks, each with two 
convolutional layers, then dropout, and max pooling. Each encoder block is followed by a feature enhancement 
attention module, which is also used in the skip connections, learning to refine the features before combining 
them with the decoder in order to provide rich context representations. A multi-scale feature extraction unit is 
present at the bottleneck, which takes in contextual information with pooled representation at various spatial 
levels, which are produced by the use of convolution and upsampling operations. The decoder also assumes a 
hierarchical architecture with every upsampling stage incorporating improved encoder characteristics through 
lateral skip connections. And these concatenated features are aimed at and polished with convolution layers. 
MAC-Net is trained by a hybrid loss that uses the Dice loss, Focal loss, and Edge loss. The architecture guarantees 
cluster-accurate region segmentation, resistance to class imbalance, and delineation of the boundaries correctly.
Hierarchical feature extraction

extract characteristic features of colorectal histopathology images at various levels of abstraction. The encoder 
includes four convolutional blocks, each consisting of two convolutional layers with kernel size 3 ×3, ReLU 
activation, and same padding, followed by a Dropout layer to regularize learning. The number of filters doubles 
at each subsequent level starting from 32, 64, 128, and 256. A MaxPooling2D operation with a 2×2 window and 
stride 2 is applied only after the first three blocks to increase the receptive field and reduce spatial dimensions.

Experiment and results
In this section, we provide a comprehensive overview of the experimental setup and present the evaluation 
results demonstrating the performance of MAC-Net.
Dataset description
The investigation in this work adopted the publicly available EBHI-Seg dataset 23, which consists of 4,452 
histopathological images: 2,226 hematoxylin and eosin (H&E)-stained colon tissue images and 2,226 
corresponding ground truth segmentation masks. TThe images were taken under 400X magnification with 
Nissan Olympus microscope and stored in 256×256 pixels in portable network graphics (PNG) format. The data 
set was carefully filtered with ethical guidance by highly qualified pathologists in the Cancer Hospital of China 
Medical University, Shenyang, Liaoning, China, and was released online. EBHI-Seg is unique in the sense that 
it includes six annotated classes of colorectal tissues consisting of diverse tumor stages such as normal, polyp, 
low-grade, and high-grade intraepithelial neoplasia (IN), serrated adenoma, and adenocarcinoma. It gives a 
detailed description of the progressive stages of CRC presented in terms of training, validation, and test sets. This 
renders the dataset applicable in training and evaluating semantic segmentation networks such as MAC-Net in a 
clinically meaningful setting. Generalizability of MAC-Net was also tested on the GIaS dataset35, comprising 165 
colon histological image samples of benign or malignant cancer, and expert-labeled gland segmentation masks. 
The GIaS images are characterized by high inter-subject variability in the distribution of the stain and structure 
of the tissue, and varied magnifications, as well as a resolution of about 0.465 µm/ pixel. This cross-dataset 
validation justifies the strength of MAC-Net in a variety of clinical imaging settings. Figure 3 demonstrate a 
sample of the respective dataset (EBHI-Seg and GIaS).
Experimental details
MAC-Net has been trained on Google Colab with a Tesla T4 16GB VRAM with the help of TensorFlow 2.12.0. 
The model was trained using a batch size of 10 and 100 epochs with the input size set at 256 ×256×3. All 
the images and masks were scaled to 256 x 256 pixels, and the min-max scaling was used to normalize to the 
range [0,1]. A fixed random seed ensured consistent augmentation across training epochs. T o ensure fair and 
reproducible comparison and division of the dataset, the benchmark was used to split the EBHI-Seg dataset into 
training, validation, and test sets in a 4:4:2 ratio. A large enough validation set minimizes chances of overfitting, 
which can happen with smaller splits (e.g., 8:1:1). Monitor validation performance and save the best-performing 
model were done by early stopping (patience =10,verbose=1), learning rate reduction on plateau (factor = 0.1, 
patience = 5, minimum learning rate = 1e-4), and model checkpointing.
Data Augmentation pipeline implemented with Keras ImageDataGenerator incorporated the following 
transformations:
• Random rotation of up to 10◦ and width and height shifts up to 5% of the original size,
• Shearing transformations with a maximum intensity of 5% and zoom variations within 5%,
• Random horizontal flipping is employed with nearest-fill mode for empty regions.
MAC-Net was compiled using Adam and a hybrid loss consisting of Focal, Dice, and Edge losses. Dice Score, 
Precision, Recall, Intersection-over-Union (IoU), and Conformity Coefficient were used to measure performance 
of the model. Interpretability was done with Grad-CAM visualizations and inference with Monte Carlo Dropout 
to produce multifold predictions for inference of both the mean and variance-based uncertainty maps.

Results and analysis of segmentation
This section provides a detailed evaluation of the proposed MAC-Net segmentation model on the EBHI-Seg 
dataset. Figure 4 illustrates the training and validation accuracy and loss curves across epochs.Training and 
validation accuracy rose significantly during the initial epochs, stabilizing after approximately 40 epochs. 
Training and validation losses decreased significantly within the first 10 epochs and continued to decline 
gradually thereafter. Training loss decreased continuously, but there was a small deviation in validation loss 
between the 20-50 epochs. Altogether, the learning curves indicate a low level of overfitting and show a steady 
convergence.
The model during training had an accuracy of 92.74%, an IoU of 91.95%, and a loss of 0.0887. When tested 
on the validation, it had an accuracy of 93.38%, a IoU of 90.21%, and a loss of 0.1101. To test, the model achieved 
an accuracy of 93.76%, a 90.55% ratio of IoU, and a loss of 0.1065.

Interpretability and uncertainty analysis
Grad-CAM visualization and Monte Carlo Dropout-based uncertainty analysis were used to test the interpretability 
and reliability of MAC-Net predictions. The resulting Grad-CAM heatmaps were scaled and superimposed onto 
the original histopathological images to show where the model in question was paying attention to during the 
segmentation process, thus pointing to the fact that the model seems to be paying attention to areas that are 
relevant to biologically meaningful tissue structures in the sample of CRC. The approach minimizes the black-
box aspects that are often attributed to the deep learning structure and contributes to the creation of a system of 
explainable AI (XAI). Figure 8 shows six representative images, one of each tumour type (Normal, Polyp, Low-
grade IN, High-grade IN, Serrated Adenoma, and Adenocarcinoma) and the associated Grad-CAM mappings.
The strong activation is represented in red-colored areas in the heatmaps, and it relates to tumoral structures. 
The model is mostly focused on densely compacted glands in adenocarcinoma, distorted gland formations or 
mucosal protrusions in serrated adenoma and polyps, and irregular glandular architecture with nuclear atypia 
in low and high-grade intraepithelial neoplasia. These areas are highlighted in line with diagnostic patterns that 
are usually studied by pathologists, implying that the model focuses on patterns occurring at tissue levels that 
are consistent with clinically meaningful ones. This interpretability can be used to refine the systematic model, 
as well as potentially aid in more transparently assessing the model in the medical setting.
Monte Carlo Dropout was additionally used on inference to measure the uncertainty of predictions. Table 4 
provides a summary of uncertainty values on test images, which are obtained by means of predictive variance. 
The small value of mean uncertainty (0.0011) shows that the model is able to make consistent predictions on 
the majority of the test samples. The uncertainty was the greatest (0.0063), which indicated that there were no 
extremely ambiguous or clear outlier cases in this test set. There is also a low standard deviation (0.0008), which 
indicates that there is very little uncertainty variation in images. The skewed distribution of the uncertainty 
values, as illustrated by Fig. 9, also proves that most of the predictions of the MAC-Net are very certain, with a 
few having a slight increase in uncertainty.
Computational complexity analysis
In order to determine the viability of investigating the implementation of MAC-Net within the resource-limited 
clinical environment, we calculated its computational efficiency. MAC-Net has 2.48 million trainable parameters 
(approximately 9.46 MB), which is quite lightweight in comparison with most of the current attention-driven 
or transformer-based networks used in segmentation 25. A layer-wise analytical FLOP estimation, based on 
multiply accumulate operations across all convolutional and dense layers, yields approximately 22.51 GFLOPs 
for a 256×256×3 input, reflecting a moderate computational load relative to the achieved accuracy. Empirical 
inference evaluation further demonstrated an average processing time of 0.6048 s per image on an NVIDIA T4

Ablation studies
A series of ablation experiments were conducted using the EBHI-Seg dataset to evaluate the individual and 
combined contributions of MAC-Net’s core components. The evaluation began with a baseline U-Net encoder–
decoder model, after which hierarchical feature extraction (H), attention refinement (A), and multi-scale 
contextual fusion (M) were introduced incrementally. Each module was individually tested in all the possible 
pairwise and complete combinations. Table 5 gives a summary of the performance of every variant.
The findings suggest that adding any one of the individual modules (H, A, and M) to the baseline U-Net 
will result in a statistically significant segmentation improvement. Additional developments are seen when 
two modules are merged. The full architecture of MAC-Net that incorporates all three modules is the most 
accurate and can be used to justify its usefulness in the process of histopathological segmenting of the CRC on 
the EBHI-Seg dataset. In Fig. 10, the visual comparison between segmentation outputs of the baseline U-Net and 
its module-augmented counterparts is shown. All rows display the various stages of the CRC, and all columns 
indicate predictions of a specific model set-up. At all times, MAC-Net generates segmentation masks that are 
much closer to the ground truth with better localization of boundaries and better structural preservation, 
compared to its U-Net variants - particularly in complicated tissue areas.
The progressive improvement of each variant points to the robustness of the unified architecture of MAC-
Net. With the hierarchical feature extraction, adaptive attention, and multi-scale contextual fusion, the model 
represents the detailed local information at the same time, preserving the higher-level structural context. 
This synergy helps to achieve more accurate gland segmentation at boundaries and enhances morphological 
continuity, where even the single-module variants are limited in the difficult tissue cases.

colorectal glands correctly, particularly in samples with proper glandular borders. The hierarchical part of

and that the integration of modules together increases both the detailed boundary detection and the contextual

Evaluation across external datasets gives information on the generalization of the models. MAC-Net retained 
high performance on EBHI-Seg and moderate performance on GIaS without retraining; the decrease in staining 
on PanNuke and Amgad demonstrates that the organ morphology, tissue characteristics, and staining variability 
affect segmentation performance. Such results suggest that MAC-Net is able to learn visual patterns on CRC 
better, yet it cannot be generalized to other types of datasets with various structural properties or nucleus-based 
attributes. This limitation could be overcome through the incorporation of multimodal data in the process of 
training, as they would offer a more diverse representation of features. The same behaviour was reported in earlier 
gland segmentation works, revealing that domain shift will continue to be a major obstacle to histopathological 
AI systems.
The uncertainty and interpretability studies help in the knowledge of model reliability. Grad-CAM images 
indicate that MAC-Net is focused on gland-rich and boundary-relevant figures, which are consistent with 
pathological decision features. The small uncertainty distribution indicates the confidence is constant in the 
majority of predictions, though the large uncertainty in the areas of complex stroma expresses that the model is 
less certain in the situations where the boundaries of the glands are ambiguous. These observances reinforce the 
importance of using the network as a decision-support tool alongside expert review rather than as a standalone 
diagnostic system.
In order to put MAC-Net perspectives into perspective, we relate its design to the latest trends in medical 
image segmentation. NHBS-Net 39, uses feature-fusion attention on neonatal hip ultrasound segmentation, 
which proves the capability of hierarchical representation combination to better segmentation in terms of 
boundary interpretation. Further, a dual-encoder 40 provides an inter-view cross-attention of low- and high-
magnification images to show both the global and fine-structure information of the tissue during the process of 
histopathological cancer analysis. MAC-Net adheres to the same architectural trend and integrates multi-scale 
representation, hierarchical context encoding, and attention-refined feature refinement into one end-to-end 
system, which, in our experiments, ensures the preservation of tissue boundaries and novel gland morphologies.
Even though MAC-Net is proposed and tested to work on the specific task of colorectal gland segmentation, 
the framework is domain-agnostic. Multi-scale feature aggregation helps the model to detect structural 
differences in the different resolutions, and contextual-attention fusion facilitates the isolation of meaningful 
and uninformative areas. These characteristics suggest potential flexibility to the other context of segmentation, 
like differentiating between true and reflective objects, which cannot be detected in a mirror 41and selecting 
saliently structured information in low-density underwater images 42. Although these applications are outside 
the clinical scope of this paper, they show an encouraging trend in the direction of future research and suggest 
that the MAC-Net can be potentially applied to non-medical segmentation tasks, although additional validation 
of the hypothesis is required.
There are several limitations that should be acknowledged. The experiment relies on two colorectal datasets, 
which is a limitation to exposure to different tissue variability. The reduction in performance observed during

facilitate the development towards reliable clinical integration and extension to other segmentation conditions

Conclusion and future work
This article introduced MAC-Net as a hybrid deep learning system that is used to segment colorectal cancer 
histopathological images. The combination of top-down feature extraction and bottom-up contextual refinement 
led to the model showing strong results on EBHI-Seg and promising results on GIaS, with deteriorating results in 
the presence of large domain variation. Grad-CAM and uncertainty mapping allowed gaining an insight into the 
behaviour of the prediction, underlining the areas that impact the model results and proving the cases when the 
prediction needs further inspection by specialists. Ablation experiments also affirmed that all the components 
played a significant role in the overall segmentation capacity. Combined, these findings indicate that MAC-
Net could function as an interpretable and efficient component within digital pathology-assisted workflows, 
provided that further validation is undertaken.
The remaining challenges will be addressed in future research. Relatively lower results with transitional tissue 
classes indicate that domain-sensitive learning of features and multimodal indications that are more attentive to 
subtle morphological changes are required. Pruning, quantization, or lightweight architectural variants can be 
used to achieve efficiency and can be deployed faster on a clinical or resource-constrained system. Massive, inter-
center validation and stain-diverse data will be necessary to guarantee high-quality generalizability between 
patient populations and imaging circumstances.
Overall, MAC-Net is ascertained to be an accurate, interpretable, and adaptable system in the reviewed field, 
which makes it a promising research basis in the field of CRC histopathological image analysis. Continuous 
work will consist of evaluating more organs, adding clinical metadata, and making the model oriented towards 
real-time diagnostic integration.
